Built in the early 13th century, this soaring tower was commissioned by Qutb-ud-din Aibak and later completed by his successor Iltutmish.

With a height of 73 meters, Qutub Minar is the tallest brick minaret in the world. Its five stories are adorned with intricate carvings of Arabic inscriptions, floral motifs, and geometric patterns ‚Äî showcasing the advanced artistry of its time.

Surrounding the minaret is the Qutub Complex, which includes the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque (India‚Äôs first mosque), the Alai Darwaza, and the mystical Iron Pillar, famous for its anti-rust composition that has baffled scientists for centuries.

Qutub Minar has withstood the tests of time, earthquakes, and invasions. It was struck by lightning and partially damaged in the 14th century but was restored by Firoz Shah Tughlaq. Today, it continues to be a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most visited attractions in Delhi.

A walk through the site offers a deep connection with India's early Islamic rule, its architectural brilliance, and the vibrant cultural history that shaped the capital city. Visiting here feels like stepping into a living museum of stone and spirit.
